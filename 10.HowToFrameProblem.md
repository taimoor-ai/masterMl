
# Framing the Machine Learning Problem

## Overview

**Framing a problem is the crucial first step in any Machine Learning project. It involves translating a vague business objective (e.g., "we want to make more money") into a specific, measurable technical task (e.g., "predict the probability of a user canceling their subscription"). While junior data scientists often receive pre-framed tasks, moving into leadership roles requires the ability to look at the "big picture" and define the roadmap from scratch.**

## Key Concepts

* **Business to ML Translation:** Converting high-level business goals into mathematical/algorithmic objectives.
* **Churn Rate:** A metric representing the percentage of customers who stop using a service over a specific period.
* **Task Identification:** Determining if a problem is Supervised (Classification/Regression) or Unsupervised.
* **Data Engineering Collaboration:** Identifying which features are needed and ensuring they can be extracted from databases.
* **Batch vs. Online Learning:** Deciding if the model should be trained periodically or updated continuously based on real-time data.

## Detailed Explanation

**The video uses a case study of **Netflix** to explain how a data scientist approaches a problem:**

* **Translating Business Problems:** A CEO might say, "Increase Revenue." A data scientist breaks this down. To increase revenue, you can acquire new customers, charge more, or reduce the number of people leaving (**Churn**). The objective becomes: **Reduce the monthly churn rate from 4.0% to 3.75%.**
* **Identifying the Task:** Initially, this looks like a **Classification** problem (Will the user stay or leave?). However, to provide tiered discounts (50% to those most likely to leave, 10% to those slightly likely), it becomes a **Regression** problem (Predicting the **probability** of leaving from 0 to 100).
* **Evaluating Current Solutions:** Before building something new, check if a baseline exists. Is there already a model predicting overall churn? What factors did it use?
* **Selecting Features (Data):** Identifying behavioral indicators of dissatisfaction, such as:

  * **Low watch time.**
  * **Frequent "Search but did not find" events.**
  * **Abandoned content (starting a show but stopping halfway).**
* **Online vs. Batch Learning:** In volatile environments like Netflix (where trends change due to lockdowns or holidays), **Online Learning** is often preferred so the model can adapt to real-time changes rather than waiting for a weekly batch update.

## Important Definitions

* **Churn Rate:** The rate at which customers leave a platform. High churn is the primary enemy of subscription-based businesses.
* **OLTP (Online Transactional Processing):** The database where live user interactions are recorded.
* **Data Warehouse (DW):** A structured repository where data is cleaned and stored specifically for analysis and ML training.
* **Inertia:** In large organizations, once a project starts in a specific direction, it is very costly and slow to change it. This is why framing must be correct at the start.

## Examples / Use Cases

### Case Study: Netflix Revenue Growth

* **Business Goal:** Increase revenue.
* **ML Solution:** Build a system to identify "At-Risk" users and offer them dynamic discounts to prevent them from unsubscribing.
* **Features Used:** User watch time, search success rate, click-through rate on recommendations.

## Step-by-Step Explanation

* **Business Problem to ML Problem:** Define the mathematical target.
* **Identify Type of Problem:** Choose between Classification, Regression, Clustering, etc.
* **Check Current Solution:** Look for existing baselines or manual heuristics.
* **Getting Data:** Collaborate with Data Engineers to source relevant features.
* **Define Metrics:** Decide exactly how "success" will be measured (e.g., RMSE for probability).
* **Online vs. Batch:** Decide the deployment and training frequency.
* **Check Assumptions:** Verify if the data is actually available and if the model applies across different regions/geographies.

## Key Takeaways

* **Leadership Skill:** Framing is what separates a senior data scientist/manager from a coder.
* **Cost of Failure:** Starting an ML project with the wrong frame is expensive and wastes months of engineering time.
* **Feature Availability:** Never assume a feature exists; always verify with the Data Engineering team.
* **Geographic Nuance:** A model that works for users in the US might not work for users in India due to different cultural behaviors.

## Practical Notes

* **As a beginner, you might not frame the whole project, but you should understand **why** you are building a specific model.**
* **Always ask: "Is this a classification task or a regression task?" and "How will the business actually use my output?"**

## Interview / Exam Points

* **Q: Why would you choose Regression over Classification for Churn?**

  * **A: Regression allows for a probability score. This helps the business prioritize interventions (discounts) based on the severity of the risk.**
* **Q: What is the risk of "Batch Learning" for a streaming service?**

  * **A: It cannot adapt quickly to sudden changes in user behavior caused by external events like a global pandemic or a viral show release.**
* **Q: What is the first thing a Data Scientist should do after receiving a business request?**

  * **A: Convert the high-level request into a measurable mathematical objective and check existing assumptions.**

## Summary

**Framing the problem is the foundation of the Machine Learning lifecycle. It requires a balance of business domain knowledge and technical expertise. By correctly defining the task, selecting the right metrics, and identifying the necessary data early on, a data scientist ensures that the resulting model actually solves the business's needs.**
